The point

Fear held steady. Hope moved with the war.

Across six years of broadcasts, fear barely moved — it ran high and jumped sharply from speech to speech, but it did not climb when the news was worst. What rose and fell with the war's fortunes was the hopeful register: anticipation, joy, and net sentiment all lifted around Allied victories and sank around defeats. Defeat was met not with despair but with resolve — danger acknowledged at a constant pitch while optimism was quietly dialed up or down.

Roosevelt and Churchill ran the same strategy in different voices. Roosevelt named danger more openly yet offered more trust and forward-looking confidence, landing more net-positive overall — the "fireside" manner, quantified. Churchill's register ran darker and less buoyant: the sound of a leader speaking to a nation under direct and immediate threat rather than one shielded by an ocean.

The takeaway is that wartime rhetoric worked as a strategic instrument, not a mirror of events. Had these broadcasts simply tracked the headlines, their emotions would have swung with every reversal; instead fear was held as a steady background note while hope did the moving. Seeing that meant treating eighty years of close-read oratory as data — and the pattern only means something when you click back to the speech that produced it.
